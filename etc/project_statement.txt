Introduction

The "Carré" is a legendary district in the heart of Liège, famous for its many bars and its
ability to attract hundreds of students every evening for a drink (or two, or three, or...) after
a long day of lectures. It has been the sacred ground of Liège students since the 1970s, and
has survived generations of arguable academic decisions.
An incredible phenomenon can be observed there after midnight: the random walking behavior shown by said students, as they clumsily attempt to make their way home on foot.
Scientists have yet to fully explain it, though leading hypotheses involve the Chouffe and a
complete misunderstanding of Euclidean geometry.
This phenomenon deserves to be modeled, and that is what this project is about. You are
invited to form groups of two1 (so you don’t get lost on the way), and to implement a game
simulating the "drunken walk" of a student leaving the Carré, encountering various obstacles
along the path home...

Technical Framework

2.1

Wayland

For this project, you are asked to use Wayland2 . Wayland is a modern protocol for Linux
systems; think of it as a middleman between your program and what actually appears on
your screen. When your code says "draw a rectangle here", Wayland is the one making sure
it ends up in the right place on your monitor.
More specifically, Wayland allows programs (called clients) to communicate with the display
server (called the compositor), which is responsible for rendering everything you see on
screen and handling input events like keyboard and mouse.
In practice, you will be working with libwayland-client, a C library that lets your program
connect to the compositor, create a window surface, and draw pixels into it.

Gameplay

The protagonist of your game is called Thomas. Thomas is walking back home from the Carré,
and is going to run into some bulky items along the way.
Your job is to help him dodge the obstacles that come his way using the arrow keys. In
practice, Thomas can move on the screen while the scenery scrolls toward him from the right;
the classic endless runner mechanic. Think Subway Surfers, but on a dirty Liège sidewalk at
2am.

The problem is... Thomas is quite drunk. Despite his efforts, he cannot help but randomly
wobble from side to side, and pitifully stumbles on random obstacles.

3.1

Game Mechanics

Obstacles
The obstacles appear every random number of seconds (up to you to calibrate it properly)
on the right of the screen, at a random vertical position, and all move at the same constant
speed. Each of them has their own probability of appearing:
• Leftover kebab: 20%,
• Garbage: 30%,
• Abandoned Bolt scooter: 10%,
• Other drunk student: 40% (on Friday night, Rue des Célestines),
Health Points
Thomas has a number of health points (maximum 100). These points are represented by the
game’s brightness: if the screen is completely bright (by default), he’s completely healthy,
and if it’s completely black, then it’s at 0/100, he passed out.
Every obstacle he runs into has a specific property:
• For each obstacle except for kebab leftovers: the screen becomes darker, because he
gets hurt.
• Leftover kebab: heals him a little bit (+10).
• Garbage: hurts him a little bit (-10).
• Abandoned Bolt scooter: hurts him a lot, as he trips and falls to the ground (-30).
• Other drunk student: hurts him a bit (-20), but he’s so embarrassed that his movements are temporarily reversed for 1 to 5 seconds (e.g., ARROW UP becomes ARROW
DOWN, ARROW LEFT becomes ARROW RIGHT, ...).
When Thomas has no health points left, the game is over. The user can then press
SPACE to restart.

Scoring
The final score corresponds to the distance traveled. At the end of the game, print the number
of seconds spent walking to the standard output (e.g., Thomas walked for 10 seconds!)
General Behavior
Pressing on arrow keys makes Thomas move for one step. The distance of a step is discrete
and yours to define.
Also, since he’s drunk, he staggers from side to side every 2 seconds on average (from his
point of view, i.e., up or down in the window) for a random number of steps between 1 and 5.
Staggering can occur at any point during the game, whether you’re moving him or not.
Regarding collisions, whenever Thomas hits anything other than kebab leftovers:
1. The screen shakes in a short (less than 1 second) and sharp way, as if the window
itself has been bumped.
2. He becomes invincible for 2 seconds (because of the adrenaline spike) and blinks
quickly during this time.
And if he finds kebab leftovers, he eats them and they disappear.

Guidelines

There are no hard constraints regarding the game’s graphical layout. The window size, exact
dimensions, and speed settings can be adjusted as you see fit. However, there are a few other
directives.

4.1

Game Structure

• You can keep the game at 30 FPS; this is more than enough to compute a frame in C++.
The FPS mechanism is already made for you in the example game.cpp file.
• At the beginning of the game, give Thomas some space so he can move forward without
being immediately showered with obstacles.
• When the game is over, you can simply keep the screen black and print GAME OVER with
the final score in stdout. Then, the player can press SPACE to restart, or ESC to close
the window.

4.2

Readability

Your code must be readable by someone who did not write it, including your graders. Examples:

4

• Organization: split your code into logical units. Each class or file should have a clear,
single responsibility. A single file called main.cpp that contains rendering, input handling, and collision logic all at once is a red flag. If you have to scroll for five minutes to
find a function, something is wrong.
• Naming: variables, functions, and classes should say what they are. x and tmp are not
names. playerPosition and obstacleSpeed are. If your name requires a comment
to explain it, the name is not good enough.
• Comments: well-written code should mostly explain itself. Use comments to explain
why something is done a certain way, not what it does: the code already says what it
does. A comment that says // increment i above i++ is either noise, or a Copilot
forgotten comment.
• Consistency: pick a naming convention (camelCase, snake_case, etc.), a brace style,
and an indentation width, and apply them uniformly throughout your entire codebase.

4.3

Robustness and good practice

The C++ standards and good practices covered throughout the semester are expected
to be applied correctly in this project and used when needed. This is an OOP C++ project,
not C.
Not exhaustively, this includes using const wherever a value is not meant to change, managing memory through smart pointers rather than raw new and delete, and respecting RAII4
so that resources are always properly acquired and released.
It also means choosing the right data structures, writing functions that do one thing and do
it well, and never letting undefined behavior slide because "it seems to work". The compiler
will not always catch your mistakes, but your graders (probably) will.

4.8

Report and AI usage

Please read the explanations below very carefully and approach them with a mature
mindset. This is not merely a set of requirements for an academic project, but also a state of
mind to be integrated into all your future projects.
The use of AI is not prohibited. However, we need a way to check that you have indeed
mastered the OOP and C++ concepts learned this semester. That is why we will ask you, on
the one hand, to present your work during a short oral presentation (see below), and on the
other hand, to write a short report (under 5 pages) explaining the overall architecture of
your project and the rationale behind your design choices. Notably, we strongly recommend
you to provide diagrams to illustrate your classes interaction (e.g., class diagrams).
Why did you build this particular class? What is the reason behind this inheritance? Were
global variables really necessary in this specific place? How do you manage the state of all
entities over time (position, size, color, etc.)? Are your data structures coherent? How is your
memory management going? etc.
Basically, show us that you understand what you are doing, and that your entire project
did not arise from a single prompt.
Since the use of AI virtually cannot be prohibited today, and is in fact becoming an almost
indispensable tool in the face of competition in the IT industry, this significantly complicates
our assessment methods, and we are counting on you to demonstrate that you have mastered
the course material as best as you can.
